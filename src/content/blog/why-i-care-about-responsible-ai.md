I learned Python during quarantine. I still remember the first time a few lines of code did something I'd asked them to — it felt a little like magic, and I was hooked. When ChatGPT came out, that feeling came back tenfold. I wanted to build AI.

But the more I learned, the more I noticed the other side of the story.

A friend told me their parent had been laid off, replaced by a model. I read about the water that cooling data centers pulls from communities that never agreed to host them, and the electricity a single large model burns through to train. I watched people reach for AI to skip the genuine effort that used to make work *theirs*.

None of this made me want to leave the field. It made me want to stay — and do it differently.

## What "responsible" means to me

I don't think the answer is to be afraid of AI, or to pretend the harms aren't real. I think the answer is to build with both hands on the wheel: to measure the costs, to ask who carries them, and to design so that the people most affected actually have a voice.

That's why I'm drawn to AI where engineering meets policy. Code decides what's *possible*; policy decides what's *allowed* and who's *protected*. I want to work on both.

## Where I'm headed

Right now I'm an incoming CS student at Stanford, an intern at the Child Poverty Action Lab, and a person who still gets that little-bit-of-magic feeling every time something I built works. I want to keep that feeling — and make sure the things I build leave the world, and the people in it, a little better off.

More to come. 🌱
